Our Story

Why we built the AI School Helper.

We noticed that many students struggle with time management and organization. While there are many apps, checking a phone often leads to distractions. We wanted a solution that was voice-first and distraction-free.

We chose the Raspberry Pi 5 because its enhanced processing power finally makes running local AI models possible. This ensures student privacy—no data leaves the device.

Our journey began with simple Python scripts and evolved into a fully integrated hardware assistant that sits on your desk, ready to help plan your day.

Mission: To provide a private, offline AI tutor for every student desk.

How it works

AI School Helper is driven by a Python assistant named Zap. Here is the full path from your voice to the answer you hear—written so anyone can follow along.

1. Wake word and recording

The Pi listens for a wake phrase (for example, “Hey Zap”). When it hears it, the USB microphone records what you say next until you pause. That gives the system one clear sentence or question to work with.

2. Speech → text

The recorded audio is run through a speech recognition model (Whisper). It turns your spoken words into plain text, similar to live captions.

3. Figuring out what you meant

The program reads that text and picks a path:

Google Calendar or Docs — If you turned on Google and your words sound like scheduling or working with a document, it calls Google’s services.

Homework planner — If you are adding, listing, or checking off homework, it updates a small database stored on the Pi.

General question — Otherwise it sends your text to a local AI (via Ollama) that runs on the Raspberry Pi, so answers do not require the cloud.

4. Building the reply

The AI writes a short answer. Any screen-style formatting (like stars used for bold) is removed so the next step does not read symbols out loud.

5. Text → speech

A voice engine (Piper) turns the final text into sound, which plays through your speaker. That is the voice you hear back.

Privacy in short

Homework and everyday questions can stay on the device. Hooking up Google for calendar or Docs is optional—you only sign in if you want those features.
